Soto Ayam

Soto Ayam is a beloved Indonesian chicken soup, celebrated for its bright yellow, turmeric-infused broth and aromatic spices. This flavorful dish combines tender shredded chicken, vermicelli or glass noodles, boiled eggs, bean sprouts, and crispy fried shallots. It is garnished with fresh herbs like celery and lime leaves, which enhance its rich aroma and taste. The addition of lime juice and sambal (chili paste) allows each serving to be customized to personal preferences, creating a balance of savory, tangy, and spicy flavors.

Typically enjoyed with a side of steamed rice or compressed rice cakes (lontong), Soto Ayam is a staple in Indonesian households and street food culture. It is not just a dish but a culinary representation of Indonesia’s diverse flavors and traditions. Whether served during family gatherings, festivals, or casual meals, Soto Ayam offers comfort and a taste of Indonesia's vibrant culinary heritage.

Siomay

Siomay is a popular Indonesian street food inspired by Chinese dim sum. It consists of steamed fish dumplings made with a mixture of fish paste, tapioca, and spices, often accompanied by boiled eggs, tofu, potatoes, and cabbage. The dish is traditionally served with a rich peanut sauce, sweet soy sauce (kecap manis), and a squeeze of lime for added zest.

This flavorful and versatile dish is commonly enjoyed as a light meal or snack throughout Indonesia. Siomay is a testament to Indonesia’s ability to blend culinary influences into something uniquely its own, offering a delicious, savory bite that is both satisfying and packed with local flavors.

Es Teh

Es Teh, or iced tea, is a refreshing and widely loved beverage in Indonesia. Made from brewed black tea, it is typically served over ice and can be enjoyed plain or sweetened with sugar. Es Teh is a staple drink found everywhere, from street vendors to high-end restaurants, offering a cooling escape in the country’s tropical climate.

This simple yet versatile drink complements a variety of Indonesian dishes, balancing spicy and savory flavors with its crisp and light taste. Whether served at family gatherings, casual meals, or traditional celebrations, Es Teh remains a timeless favorite, symbolizing warmth and hospitality.

Es Timun Selasih

Es Timun Selasih is a refreshing Indonesian drink made with shredded cucumber, basil seeds (selasih), simple syrup, and chilled water or ice. Often enhanced with a splash of lime or lemon juice, this drink offers a perfect balance of sweetness and tanginess, making it an ideal thirst-quencher in the tropical heat.

Loved for its unique combination of textures, Es Timun Selasih combines the crunchiness of cucumber with the jelly-like consistency of soaked basil seeds. Commonly served at traditional events or as a cooling treat during Ramadan, this drink showcases Indonesia’s creativity in blending simple ingredients into delightful and refreshing beverages.

Ingredients:
  • 1 whole chicken (cut into pieces)
  • 1 tablespoon turmeric powder (or fresh turmeric)
  • 5 cloves garlic (minced)
  • 4 shallots (minced)
  • 2 stalks lemongrass (bruised)
  • 3-4 kaffir lime leaves
  • 1 tablespoon ginger (minced)
  • 200g vermicelli noodles (or rice noodles)
  • 2 boiled eggs (sliced)
  • 2 boiled potatoes (optional, cubed)
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 1 tablespoon vegetable oil
  • 1 liter chicken broth (or water)
  • Fresh cilantro leaves (for garnish)
  • Fried shallots (for garnish)
  • Lime wedges (for serving)
  • Soy sauce or sambal (optional, for serving)
Steps:
  1. Prepare the chicken: Boil the chicken in a large pot of water until cooked, around 30-40 minutes. Once done, remove the chicken, set aside to cool, then shred the meat.
  2. Prepare the broth: In another pot, heat the vegetable oil and sauté minced garlic, shallots, turmeric, ginger, and lemongrass until fragrant. Add the boiled chicken stock (or water) to the pot, and stir. Let it simmer for 10-15 minutes to infuse the flavors. Add the lime leaves and salt to taste.
  3. Prepare the noodles: Boil the vermicelli noodles according to the package instructions. Drain and divide into serving bowls.
  4. Assemble the Soto: Add the shredded chicken, boiled potatoes (optional), and boiled eggs to each bowl. Pour the hot broth over the noodles and toppings.
  5. Garnish and serve: Garnish with fresh cilantro, fried shallots, and a squeeze of lime juice. Serve with lime wedges, soy sauce, and sambal for extra flavor.
Ingredients:
  • 500g fish fillet (commonly tilapia, mackerel, or chicken)
  • 100g tapioca flour
  • 1 egg
  • 1 tablespoon garlic (minced)
  • 1 tablespoon shallots (minced)
  • 1 tablespoon soy sauce
  • 1 teaspoon sugar
  • Salt and pepper to taste
  • 200g tofu (cut into cubes)
  • 200g boiled potatoes (sliced)
  • 3-4 boiled eggs (peeled and halved)
  • 200g cabbage leaves (blanched)
Peanut Sauce (for serving):
  • 200g roasted peanuts (ground)
  • 3 tablespoons sweet soy sauce
  • 1-2 cloves garlic
  • 1 tablespoon chili paste (optional)
  • Water to adjust consistency
Steps:
  1. Prepare the fish paste: Blend the fish fillet in a food processor with tapioca flour, egg, garlic, shallots, soy sauce, sugar, salt, and pepper. Blend until the mixture is smooth and elastic.
  2. Prepare the filling: Shape the fish paste into small dumplings, about the size of a golf ball. You can stuff the dumplings into cabbage leaves, tofu cubes, and place a half-boiled egg inside each.
  3. Steam the dumplings: Arrange the prepared siomay (dumplings) in a steamer basket and steam for 20-30 minutes, or until they are fully cooked.
  4. Prepare the peanut sauce: In a blender, combine the ground roasted peanuts, sweet soy sauce, garlic, chili paste (optional), and a little water. Blend until smooth. Adjust with more water for your desired consistency.
  5. Assemble and serve: Once the siomay is done, serve it on a platter with boiled potatoes, tofu, eggs, and cabbage. Drizzle with peanut sauce and garnish with fried shallots and a squeeze of lime.
Ingredients:
  • 2 bags of black tea (or 2 tablespoons loose leaf tea)
  • 500ml hot water
  • 3-4 tablespoons sugar (adjust to taste)
  • Ice cubes
  • Lemon wedges (optional, for garnish)
  • Fresh mint leaves (optional, for garnish)
Steps:
  1. Prepare the tea: Boil 500ml of water and pour it over the tea bags (or loose leaf tea) in a teapot or heatproof container. Let it steep for 3-5 minutes, depending on how strong you want the tea.
  2. Sweeten the tea: Remove the tea bags or strain the tea leaves, then stir in sugar while the tea is still hot. Adjust the sugar according to your sweetness preference.
  3. Chill the tea: Let the tea cool down to room temperature, then place it in the refrigerator to chill for at least 30 minutes.
  4. Serve: Fill a glass with ice cubes and pour the chilled tea over the ice.
  5. Garnish and serve: Optionally, garnish with a slice of lemon and a sprig of fresh mint for extra flavor. Serve cold and enjoy!
Ingredients:
  • 1 medium cucumber (peeled and julienned or diced)
  • 2 tablespoons basil seeds (selasih seeds)
  • 500ml water
  • 2 tablespoons sugar (adjust to taste)
  • 1 tablespoon lemon juice (optional, for added tang)
  • Ice cubes
  • Fresh mint leaves (optional, for garnish)
  • 2-3 pandan leaves (optional, for fragrance)
Steps:
  1. Prepare the basil seeds: Soak the basil seeds in water for about 10 minutes, or until they expand and become gelatinous.
  2. Prepare the cucumber: Peel the cucumber, then julienne or dice it into small pieces. Set aside.
  3. Make the sweet syrup: In a small saucepan, heat 500ml of water with sugar until the sugar dissolves completely. Add pandan leaves if using, and let it simmer for 5 minutes for extra fragrance. Remove pandan leaves and let the syrup cool down.
  4. Assemble the drink: In a glass, combine the prepared cucumber, soaked basil seeds, and sweet syrup. Add lemon juice if you prefer a tangy taste.
  5. Serve: Add ice cubes to the glass and garnish with fresh mint leaves if desired. Stir well and enjoy the refreshing Es Timun Selasih!
